Executive summary

The Akhadana, Rajasthan plant is a 360 MW ground-mounted utility asset in Rajasthan’s Thar-edge exposure where dust, water scarcity, and array scale broke the manual washing model. Taypro deployed 80 NYUMA semi-automatic robots (~0.22 robots/MW, semi-automatic-first) under CAPEX ownership, implementing mega-scale semi-automatic portability when autonomy paths are still maturing.

Operations attribute roughly 50.4 million litres of water saved per year, improved cleaning cadence, about 13.50 GWh of additional clean generation, and 6,696 metric tons CO₂ equivalent with consistent grid factors. This case study is written for owners, O&M leads, and technical advisors evaluating robotic cleaning at utility scale, with statistics, procurement lessons, and internal links to Taypro products and peer deployments.

Regional soiling and performance ratio economics

Rajasthan’s Thar-edge exposure produces fast dust return on module glass. Fine particulate bonds during long dry spells; short rains may rinse some blocks while leaving mud spotting on downwind edges and haul-road-facing strings. At 360 MW, a sustained half-point performance ratio (PR) drift is a material annual MWh event, owners care whether cleaning is programmed, logged, and correlated with recovery, not whether glass looks acceptable from a service road.

Before Taypro, manual programmes struggled with frequency, water logistics, and auditability. Tanker dependence and crew mobilisation across remote tables could not match dust season; supervisors lacked block-level proof when finance asked why generation moved month to month.

Fleet design philosophy at 360 MW

Rather than spreading a thin automatic fleet across every hectare on day one, the owner sized 80 NYUMA semi-automatic portables (~0.22 robots/MW) to cover high-return blocks with waterless brushing while civil layouts matured. Semi-automatic density is a capital allocation choice: fewer machines than marketing “one robot per MW” slides, deployed where marginal MWh per pass is highest. Why Akhadana chose eighty semi-automatic machines on 360 MW

At 360 megawatts, the intuitive procurement slide is often “more robots equals more cleanliness.” Akhadana inverted that logic: the owner deployed eighty NYUMA semi-automatic portables, about 0.22 robots per MW, because the near-term goal was verified waterless coverage on the highest-return blocks, not uniform scheduled GLYDE paths across every hectare on day one. Rajasthan’s Thar-edge dust does not wait for civil layouts to become robot-perfect; PR drifts on downwind berms and haul-road strings while teams debate whether autonomy is mature enough.

Semi-automatic waterless brushing lets crews close those pockets quickly without tanker logistics. Each portable unit is staged where drive time stays low; supervisors publish weekly block queues; inspection sheets record completion when NECTYR-style telematics were not yet the primary layer at commissioning in 2021. The discipline is old-school operations executed with modern hardware: start/stop ownership, brush care, wind holds, and signed routes.

Compare that philosophy to Bachau DVC’s 172 GLYDE automatic machines on 300 MW (~0.57 robots/MW with NECTYR dashboards) or Agar’s 272-robot mixed fleet on 200 MW. Akhadana proves mega-scale can mean focused capital intensity, not maximum robot count.

Dust-season war room: how eighty portables stay on schedule

March through June at Akhadana is not a single “cleaning campaign”, it is a weekly war room where supervisors reconcile weather forecasts, crew availability, spare brush inventory, and inverter trend flags. Because telematics were not the primary layer at launch, the war room runs on paper and spreadsheets that must be as disciplined as NECTYR dashboards: every block has a named owner, every missed night has a reschedule date, and every partial pass is recorded honestly.

Downwind berms and haul-road strings are cleaned first not because they are easiest but because SCADA shows they pay back fastest. Interior blocks enter the queue when marginal MWh per pass remains above the threshold owners set with finance. That threshold should be documented, if it is oral, robot programmes lose budget the first time curtailment compresses hours.

Technicians carry spare brush sets in support vehicles sized for eighty machines, not for a pilot of five. A single worn brush on a downwind string can cost more MWh than a week of interior passes skipped, supervisors treat brush wear as fleet risk, not consumable noise.

Upgrading from inspection-led ops to fleet dashboards

Owners reading this case study in 2026 may upgrade Akhadana with NECTYR without replacing NYUMA hardware. The upgrade path is easier when inspection sheets already list block IDs consistently, dashboards amplify discipline that exists; they do not create discipline from chaos.

When comparing upgrade ROI, model supervisor hours saved and faster root-cause meetings, not only “digital transformation” language. Pair upgrades with performance methodology acceptance tests on a pilot block before plant-wide rollout.

Supervisor playbook: weekly block queues at 360 MW

Each Monday, supervisors publish a seven-day block queue ranked by marginal MWh per pass, not alphabetically by block name. The queue is visible to electrical, civil, and portable crews so lockouts and robot nights do not collide. When curtailment compresses hours, the queue shrinks to downwind haul roads and quarry-adjacent strings, interior blocks wait with documented dates, not vague “next week.”

Technicians sign inspection sheets with block IDs finance recognises. Partial passes are recorded honestly; reschedules carry dates. When inverter trends soften on a block logged clean, run a five-why: brush wear, incomplete coverage, equipment fault, or curtailment, not generic weather excuses.

Executive summary

The Agar solar plant in Madhya Pradesh is a 200 megawatt (MW) ground-mounted utility asset where dust, water scarcity, and array scale made manual washing economically and operationally unsustainable. Taypro deployed a mixed robotic fleet—265 GLYDE automatic cleaners plus seven semi-automatic units, 272 machines in total—so the owner could run frequent waterless dry cleaning with block-level proof of completion instead of scaling night labour to match hundreds of hectares of DC capacity.

Since the robotic programme was commissioned in 2024, site operations attribute roughly 28 million litres of water saved per year, improved cleaning cadence, about 7.5 gigawatt-hours (GWh) of additional clean generation, and roughly 3,720 metric tons of CO₂ equivalent when recovered MWh are valued against grid emission factors. Fleet density is approximately 1.36 robots per MW (272 robots on 200 MW), higher than many marketing benchmarks because the owner prioritised cycle frequency and logged coverage over minimising robot count on paper.

This case study is written for owners, O&M leads, and technical advisors evaluating automatic and semi-automatic programmes on large Indian plants. It explains regional soiling, what failed in the manual era, how GLYDE and NYUMA-class systems were combined, how NECTYR-style monitoring changed accountability, and how to benchmark Agar against peer deployments such as the 300 MW Bachau DVC automatic programme or the 200 MW SECI-2 mixed fleet.

Geography, soiling, and why performance ratio drifts

Agar sits in a high-dust, semi-arid belt of Madhya Pradesh. Fine particulate accumulates on module glass through long dry intervals; short rains can rinse some blocks but often leave mud spotting along access corridors and downwind edges. In utility operations, soiling rarely degrades every string equally—invertor loading on border rows and roads-facing blocks typically softens days before a casual visual inspection would justify a plant-wide wash campaign.

At 200 MW, even a modest sustained soiling loss percentage translates into material MWh. Owners therefore care less about “clean-looking glass” and more about whether cleaning is programmed, logged, and correlated with PR recovery. That framing is what pushed Agar from episodic manual washing toward a robotic utility comparable to inverter PM: recurring, measured, escalated when missed.

Peers in similar dust corridors—Rajasthan and Gujarat mega plants, or other Madhya Pradesh sites—face the same physics. The difference at Agar is the combination of mixed autonomy and dashboard accountability, not a unique climate exemption. O&M before robotics: water, labour, and proof gaps

Before Taypro, manual cleaning could not scale with block count. Wet washing implied tanker logistics, runoff management, and crew mobilisation across a remote site where the nearest reliable water source is far from the array. Labour was costly to schedule through peak soiling weeks, and completion was not recorded per block—supervisors struggled to explain month-to-month generation variance to finance or lenders.

Three failure modes dominated internal reviews. Frequency failure: cycles were too infrequent relative to dust return, so PR drift compounded across strings. Water failure: acute scarcity made deep wet washes a planning exercise, not a routine O&M action. Audit failure: without timestamps and paths, “we cleaned last week” could not survive technical due diligence. Robotics were evaluated to close all three without flooding modules or hiring proportional night crews every season.

Owners comparing manual versus robotic economics should document historical wash spend, tanker counts, and crew headcount—not only nameplate MW. Agar’s baseline implied that manual programmes were already expensive yet still insufficient for PR stability.

Fleet design: 265 automatic GLYDE units and seven semi-automatic gaps

Taypro sized a predominantly automatic programme for repeatable ground-mount rows. 265 GLYDE automatic robots execute GLYDE scheduled paths when weather and block timers allow where geometry repeats; seven semi-automatic machines close transitions, irregular blocks, or zones where full autonomy was still being onboarded. The 265:7 split is intentional engineering, not a compromise—automatic coverage maximises scheduled-cycle throughput; semi-automatic coverage prevents dusty pockets that undermine portfolio-level PR goals.

GLYDE-class systems use soft brush contact, controlled traverse speeds, and docking/charging plans sized for the plant’s row kilometres. NYUMA-class semi-automatic units share the waterless philosophy but fit portable coverage models where civil layout or staging still limits full scheduled GLYDE paths. Together they implement mixed-mode cleaning described on Taypro’s product overview and detailed on the automatic and semi-automatic product pages.

At ~1.36 robots per MW, Agar carries more assets than a 300 MW plant with 172 automatic-only machines (~0.57 robots/MW) such as Bachau DVC. The comparison is not “better or worse”—it reflects different row repeatability and owner preference for coverage intensity versus capital concentration.

NECTYR monitoring and day-to-day accountability

Procurement was CAPEX: robots and core accessories on the plant balance sheet, with Taypro commissioning, training, and spare support. Operations lean on NECTYR fleet visibility—automated cycle scheduling, remote performance snapshots, and alert management—so day staff see which blocks completed scheduled cycles, when wind holds paused runs, and which machines need brush or drive attention.

That visibility changes weekly meetings. Instead of debating whether cleaning happened, supervisors review completion maps and reschedule missed blocks before PR bleeds into month-end SCADA. Wind holds are enforced; brush wear is tracked; tier-one faults are cleared locally. The culture mirrors inverter defect tickets: logged, owned, closed.

Plants without dashboards yet can still learn from Agar’s discipline: the underlying requirement is block-level proof, whether the interface is NECTYR or rigorous inspection protocols until telematics expand.

Cleaning cadence: scheduled cycles and weather-aware holds

Taypro GLYDE fleets do not imply a naive “every module, every night” wash. Each unit is assigned to an array with a docking station and runs scheduled waterless cycles in block-wise windows—typically post-sunset or pre-sunrise, outside peak generation—configured in NECTYR. Plant studies and seasonal soiling set how many runs occur per month; Taypro’s utility programmes commonly align with roughly 3–10 dry-cleaning cycles per month, often denser in peak dust season (for example 6–10) and lighter in quieter months, as described on cleaning technology and cleaning service pages.

AI- and ML-informed scheduling in NECTYR combines weather forecasts, rain probability, wind limits, and fleet telemetry. After effective rain, robots often stand down to avoid redundant passes; after dust fronts, schedules tighten so performance ratio recovers before the next revenue-critical period. Operators see the same weather context used for wind holds—overrides should be informed, not blind.

Each completed automatic run is a dual-pass waterless cycle on fixed ground-mount tables, with dust removal quoted per completed cycle under performance methodology—not nameplate efficiency gain and not guaranteed daily coverage of the full DC table.

Commissioning, training, and handover

Commissioning was phased. Paths were walked on energised rows where safety rules allowed; end-of-row turns, cable management zones, and inverter-yard proximity were validated before production cleaning. Parking and charging hubs were placed to minimise deadhead time between blocks—critical when 272 assets must share approved cleaning windows.

Local technicians trained on start/stop, brush inspection, fault codes, and wind-hold rules before Taypro demobilised. Handover included path maps, escalation contacts, and spare reorder thresholds sized for Rajasthan-class dust abrasiveness applied to Madhya Pradesh conditions. Robotic cleaning was inserted into the existing O&M calendar beside vegetation control and thermal scans so it did not live as a side project.

Water economics: 28 million litres avoided

Site operations report about 28 million litres of water avoided annually versus wet-wash baselines. At 200 MW in a water-stressed region, that avoidance is both an OPEX line item and a logistics unlock: fewer tanker convoys, less runoff handling, and reduced conflict between washing and electrical maintenance windows.

Dry brushing at night also avoids thermal shock from midday sprays. The point is not that water never touches the site for human consumption or civil needs—it is that module cleaning no longer depends on flooding rows on a repeating schedule.

When building a business case, finance should pair water savings with labour stability and generation attribution rather than treating water alone as the ROI driver.

Generation and carbon: 7.5 GWh and 3,720 tCO₂e

Reported additional clean generation is about 7.5 GWh per year, with roughly 3,720 metric tons CO₂ equivalent when grid factors are applied consistently. Dividing 7.5 GWh by 200 MW nameplate illustrates intensity (~37.5 MWh/MW-year if taken literally), but owners should attribute conservatively: curtailment, weather, and equipment outages belong in the same SCADA narrative.

The operational win Agar cites first is often PR stability—fewer emergency wash calls, fewer unexplained dips on downwind blocks, and faster root-cause splits between soiling and inverter faults because cleaning logs exist.

Technology, safety, and brush compliance

Waterless robotic cleaning succeeds only when brush materials, traverse speeds, and hold logic respect OEM warranty guidance. Agar’s acceptance criteria included path repeatability, wind interlocks, and technician ownership—topics covered in Taypro’s cleaning technology overview and performance methodology pages.

Safety sign-off covered night traffic near inverter yards and lockout coordination. Robots do not run in parallel with energized maintenance in the same block; windows are published in the O&M calendar.

Who should use Agar as a benchmark?

Owners with 150–250 MW of mostly repeatable ground mount, water constraints, and a need for dashboard accountability—not plants with 100% irregular rooftop geometry or tracker-only layouts without semi-automatic planning.

How many robots per MW are “enough”?

There is no universal number. Agar’s ~1.36 robots/MW reflects coverage intensity; Bachau’s ~0.57 reflects a different layout maturity. Model with row kilometres and SCADA, not brochures.

CAPEX vs Opex service?

Agar chose CAPEX. Owners wanting a managed service model should review Opex cleaning service separately; robotics hardware may be similar while commercial terms differ.

What should we verify in SCADA before claiming MWh uplift?

Align reported 7.5 GWh with curtailment logs, inverter availability, and weather-normalised PR. Use Agar as a cleaning baseline story, not a substitute for plant-level attribution models.

Monthly operating calendar and seasonal intensity

Robotic cleaning at Agar is planned month by month, not treated as a reactive wash when PR collapses. January–February: review brush wear after winter dryness; validate hold thresholds after any storm fronts. March–June: peak dust season—scheduled cleaning windows are fully utilised, and NECTYR completion rates are tracked daily because marginal MWh recovery per pass is highest. Monsoon transition: lighter passes and inspection-heavy weeks when rain naturally rinses some blocks; robots avoid unsafe wind and saturated soil near access roads. Post-monsoon: re-verify paths after vegetation cutting or civil works; update maps when rows shift.

This calendar is how 272 machines remain useful without “running everywhere equally.” Supervisors maintain a priority queue: downwind haul roads, quarry-adjacent strings, then interior blocks with stable soiling slopes. The queue is visible in monitoring—not tribal knowledge carried by one lead.

Correlating inverter data with cleaning logs

At 200 MW, string-level trends often flag soiling before plant security cameras show dirty glass. Agar’s O&M team pairs inverter loading snapshots with block completion timestamps. If a block was logged clean and PR remains soft, escalation goes to brush wear, partial path coverage, or equipment fault—not automatic blame on “soiling anyway.”

That discipline shortens root-cause meetings. It also protects robotics budget during annual reviews: finance sees cleaning as a measurable programme tied to SCADA, not a opaque maintenance line item.

Spares, consumables, and lifecycle cost

Brush sets, drive components, and battery health drive lifecycle cost as much as robot capital. Agar sized spares for March–June utilisation peaks—when missing one brush set can idle an automatic unit for three nights and silently cost MWh across dozens of hectares. Preventive maintenance intervals follow Taypro guidance; local technicians handle tier-one responses; remote support clears exceptions.

Owners building CAPEX models should include consumables and training time, not only hardware purchase price. The ROI calculator is a starting point; site-specific row kilometres refine it.

Block coverage math at 200 MW scale

If a productive night covers a fraction of total block groups, supervisors model how many nights are required to cycle the full 200 MW footprint. With 272 robots, Agar targets high-return blocks first when hours are limited—an operations research problem, not a marketing robots-per-MW slogan. Plants with only 0.5 robots/MW may still win on MWh if their layout is more repeatable; Agar chose higher density because dust return windows are short.

Does mixed fleet increase training burden?

Yes—but deliberately. Technicians learn automatic path supervision and semi-automatic portable coverage. The seven semi-automatic units are insurance against geometry gaps, not a sign the automatic programme failed.

Where do semi-automatic machines park and charge?

Procurement checklist for 200 MW mixed fleets

Technical committees evaluating Agar-like programmes should request row-level maps, not only MW totals. Ask vendors for: path repeatability percentage, count of blocks requiring semi-automatic coverage in year one, docking locations, night-window hours after curtailment, and spare lead times for brush sets. Compare GLYDE automatic coverage plans with NYUMA-class semi-automatic staging for gaps.

Legal and ESG reviewers should see NECTYR sample exports—block completion timestamps, hold reasons, and idle diagnostics—before CAPEX approval. Agar’s lesson is that robotics ROI is as much about auditability as about brushes.

Night operations, curtailment, and grid coordination

Utility-scale cleaning competes for the same calm nights as other O&M tasks. Agar schedules robotics after vegetation and civil windows are published, and coordinates with grid operators when export limits change available hours. Curtailment does not remove soiling—it compresses cleaning time—so supervisors prioritise blocks with the highest marginal MWh per pass when hours shrink.

Technicians document any block skipped because of export limits separately from wind holds, so month-end reviews do not confuse grid events with fleet faults.

Training curriculum and knowledge retention

With 272 assets, turnover is inevitable. Agar’s handover pack includes laminated fault trees, brush wear photos from local dust, and escalation paths to Taypro remote support. New hires shadow two full night cycles before solo sign-off. Semi-automatic crews rotate through automatic supervision weeks so the 265:7 split does not become two disconnected teams.

Owners without a training plan often under-use semi-automatic insurance—treating seven portables as spare parts instead of active PR protection.

Madhya Pradesh grid context and seasonal dust fronts

MP utility plants see sharp dust fronts after dry spells and local traffic on haul roads. Agar supervisors tag weather days in NECTYR notes so month-end reviews separate meteorology from fleet under-performance. When fronts pass, priority blocks reset to downwind edges before interior tables—matching how soiling actually arrives, not how blocks appear on a map alphabetically.

Coordination with utility operations teams keeps cleaning off curtailment-critical nights without abandoning pre-monsoon targets.

Mixed-fleet coordination between GLYDE and semi-automatic crews

GLYDE automatic paths and seven semi-automatic zones share approved cleaning windows but not identical fault trees. Agar publishes a single O&M calendar showing which blocks are automatic-only versus portable-required so crews do not duplicate passes or skip transitions. Radio discipline matters at 272 assets—confusion on transition blocks is a common source of PR bleed on mixed plants.

Monthly reviews compare automatic completion maps in NECTYR with semi-automatic inspection sheets for gap blocks. Gaps trigger path updates or semi-automatic redeployment within 48 hours during dust season, not at quarter-end.
